High-level philosophy

The repo should demonstrate three things: (1) you can decompose agent behavior into measurable properties, (2) you can build infrastructure that supports iterative research (not just pass/fail CI), and (3) you think carefully about what "correct" means when ground truth is fuzzy. That last point is the heart of alignment eval work.

What each part should actually contain:
evals/base.py — An Eval abstract class with a clean contract: takes a model, produces a Result with per-example scores, metadata, and traces. This is the piece that signals "I've thought about this as infrastructure, not scripts."

evals/grounding/ — Given your aviation work, this is your strongest showcase. Measures whether generated claims are supported by retrieved context. Include at least two graders (e.g., NLI-based + LLM judge) and report agreement between them. The interesting research question: when they disagree, which is right?

evals/robustness/ — Prompt perturbations (paraphrase, typos, injected distractors, jailbreak attempts). This is where you signal adversarial robustness interest. Measure score degradation under perturbation, not just raw accuracy.

evals/tool_use/ — For an agent, this matters a lot: did it pick the right tool, with correct arguments, in the right order? Include traces where the agent chose a plausible-but-wrong tool — those are the interesting failures.

graders/llm_judge.py — Don't just have an LLM score outputs. Include a calibration study: run the judge against human labels on a subset, report agreement (Cohen's kappa or similar), and discuss where the judge systematically disagrees with humans. This alone puts you ahead of most eval repos.

analysis/significance.py — Bootstrap confidence intervals on your metrics. Most eval harnesses report a single number; showing you know 73% ± 4% matters more than 73% demonstrates statistical maturity.

analysis/failure_clustering.py — Embed failed examples and cluster them. Produce a short report like "model X fails disproportionately on examples involving numerical reasoning in regulatory language." This is qualitative research, and it's what actually drives iteration.

experiments/YYYY-MM-description/ — Each run has a config, a results file, and a short markdown writeup: hypothesis, setup, result, what changed, what to try next. Treating experiments as first-class artifacts signals research mindset.

docs/methodology.md — 2–4 pages explaining: what behaviors you're measuring, why those and not others, how cases were constructed, what you're explicitly not measuring. This is the document a researcher will actually read to decide if you know what you're doing.

What to sanitize before publishing:
Obviously the aviation data and any client-specific prompts. Replace with a small synthetic analog — pick a public domain (FDA drug labels, building codes, SEC filings) and build 50–100 grounding cases on that. The methodology transfers; the data doesn't need to.

The README is the whole game
Most people won't read the code. The README should have:

* One-paragraph pitch — what problem, what approach, what's novel
* A results table — models × evals, with confidence intervals
* A "known limitations" section — this signals research honesty more than any benchmark score
* One surprising finding — "LLM judges agreed with humans 89% of the time overall, but only 61% on refusal cases; we investigated why" — this is what gets shared on Twitter and read by reviewers
